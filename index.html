Gender impacts Sleep

Studies show that women require more sleep than men do, but their sleep quality is lower than men.

According to a survey on sleep habits, women reported more issues with their sleep than did men.

Over the course of a lifetime, these sleep complaints contribute to lost sleep.

Sleep & Race

Non-Hispanic White women averaged 387 min of sleep, whereas Minority women averaged 375 min.

According to Dr. Chandra Jackson, a researcher with The Social and Environmental Determinants of Health Equity Group, "many people of color have historically lived and continue to largely live in disadvantaged, obesogenic physical and social environments," and as such, "these greater adverse exposures likely negatively affect sleep, resulting in physiological dysregulation. Physiological dysregulation may, in turn, lead to increased obesity risk and subsequent health consequences, which are likely more influential than potential genetic differences in race, a social construct." We explore some of these environmental factors in later sections.


Sleep & Obesity

Women who are overweight or obese are more likely to have a sleep disorder than women who are normal weight.

There is a bidirectional relationship between sleep and obesity - those who are already obese are more likely to have poorer sleep, and those with poorer sleep are more likely to become obese. There are numerous proposed mechanisms that contribute to this relationship.

Women getting less than the recommended amount of sleep (fewer than 8.4 hours each night) report being too sleepy to eat healthy foods or too sleepy to exercise, two behaviors that directly contribute to body weight.

Access to healthy foods and indoor or outdoor facilitites to exercise can also be impacted by the neighborhood a woman lives in.

For example, a food desert is an area, especially one with low-income residents, that has limited access to affordable and nutritious food. In contrast, an area with supermarkets or vegetable shops is a food oasis (citation needed). When tired due to insufficient sleep, it is even harder to find the motivation to seek out healthy food options.

Sleep & Financial Stability

Low-income women are especially likely to report being kept up at bedtime by worries about financial matters.

The majority of women report never being kept awake at night worrying about financial matters, but for women from households that make under $15k, they are more likely to worry about financial matters at bedtime, and if they report worrying every day, they are more than twice as likely to report taking over an hour to fall asleep.

Sleep & Comorbidities

A mental health condition like depression increases the variability of sleep.

Most women without a health condition report good sleep almost every night with a very short sleep onset latency (less than 15 minutes). Women with a mental health condition (like depression) have much more variable sleep quality and a long sleep latency contributes to reporting poor sleep quality.

In contrast, sleep is less variable for women with high blood pressure.

Having high blood pressure doesn’t contribute as much to the likelihood of having poor sleep - this is a condition that might be easier to manage in relation to sleep than some others like anxiety and depression, for example, which often have symptomology directly related to sleep. Blood pressure conditions might be easier for certain populations (low SES, racial minorities, etc.) to get treatment for than mental health conditions like depression and anxiety (citation needed)

Tip 1: Evaluate your Technology Usage

Even though there are certain things that you can't change that can impact your sleep, like your race, ethnicity, socioeconomic status, or current neighborhood of residence, there are still many things you can do to make meaningful changes in your life that can help to improve your sleep for the better.

Women who reported always turning off their tablet, television, or cell phone prior to sleep also reported substantially higher sleep quality than those who reported sometimes leaving these devices on.


Tip 2: Consult a doctor to learn how to use medicine correctly

Both over-the-counter medications and prescribed sleep medications may help women with sleep problems when used correctly.

Tip 3: Reflect on your relationship

Sleep quality can also depend on relationship satisfaction.

Women who reported being very satisfied with their relationship with their partner also reported having good sleep every night or almost every night. If you aren’t having very good sleep right now, consider how your interpersonal relationships may be impacting your sleep, especially if you share a bed with your partner. Unresolved issues with a partner may result in worrying or anxiety at bedtime, or may manifest in less-than-ideal sleeping situations (e.g., if partners are fighting and one is asked to sleep on the couch instead of in the bed).
