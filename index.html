Women & Sleep



According to researchers at the National Heart, Lung, & Blood Institute, quality sleep is pivotal to several aspects of physical & mental well-being, such as immune system functioning & memory formation. However, the Center for Disease Control & Prevention has found that only one-third of adults get the sleep they need to maintain healthy bodies & minds. While it is common knowledge that sleep is important, there remains a lack of public awareness around how sleep affects women in particular. Strikingly, for example, the Office on Women’s Health reports that women are more likely than men to experience sleep issues such as insomnia & restless leg syndrome. We hope to shed light on how women experience sleep & sleep disorders.

Sleep & Gender

According to a survey on sleep habits, women reported more issues with their sleep than did men.




Perhaps to help cope with these sleep complaints, women report higher usage of prescription sleep aids.

However, this countermeasure may not be enough, & difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or waking too early may become worse as a woman ages. Over the course of her lifetime, these minutes of lost sleep accumulate to significant loss.






A woman's sleep changes across several developmental milestones, with the potential for negative impacts on her sleep quality. According to seminal research conducted by Dr. Mary Carskadon, adolescents' internal sleep clocks shift later during puberty, making early risetimes during high school & college more difficult; hormonal changes during pregnancy as well as postpartum care of the infant cause sleep fragmentation; & lastly, symptoms of menopause also detract from a woman's sleep quality.


Sleep & Intersectionality

Intersectionality is a theory that suggests that "the overlap of various social identities, such as race, gender, sexuality, & class, contributes to the specific type of systemic oppression & discrimination experienced by an individual."


Intersectionality is especially relevant when considering the additional possible identities a woman might have that further disadvantage her sleep.




Individuals living in the Southeast United States search “insomnia” more than people in other areas, & this region also exhibits intersectionality of gender (primarily female), race (primarily African American & Black) & socioeconomic status (primarily lower-income).





Though more White women than Black or Asian women appear to suffer from insomnia, this may not be entirely representative, as cases may be undiagnosed, especially if socioeconomic factors decrease a woman's access to medical care.


Sleep apnea, a sleep disorder that is in part associated with obesity, is significantly higher in Black woman compared to White or Asian women.

According to Dr. Chandra Jackson, a researcher with The Social & Environmental Determinants of Health Equity Group, "many people of color have historically lived & continue to largely live in disadvantaged, obesogenic physical & social environments," & as such, "these greater adverse exposures likely negatively affect sleep, resulting in physiological dysregulation. Physiological dysregulation may, in turn, lead to increased obesity risk & subsequent health consequences, which are likely more influential than potential genetic differences in race, [which is] a social construct."



The bidirectional relationship between sleep & obesity makes it such that women who are already overweight or obese are more likely to develop a sleep disorder, & having a sleep disorder increases a woman's likelihood to become overweight or obese.







Diet & exercise habits directly contribute to body weight, but women who get less than the recommended amount of sleep (8.4 hours per night) report being too sleepy to eat healthy foods or too sleepy to exercise.


The ability to purchase healthy food & access exercise opportunities depends both on the offerings of one's neighborhood & one's financial assets. In addition to being related to obesity risks, socioeconomic status also has the ability to negatively impact women's sleep by fostering worrisome thoughts at bedtime.




Women living in low-income households (less than $25k) are especially likely to report disturbed sleep as a result of worrying about costs related to rent, gas, or food.

Of these women who worry about financial matters at bedtime, the low income women take longer to fall asleep.

Sleep & Comorbidities

In addition to intersectional identities, women's sleep may also be negatively impacted by other medical conditions. Mood disorders, such as depression & anxiety, have symptomology that are in part characterized by sleep disturbances such as insomnia, hypersomnia, frequent nighttime awakenings, & delayed sleep onset.




Breathing disorders, such as asthma or emphysema, impact women both while awake & while asleep. This interaction of breathing challenges & sleep disorders can be especially dangerous when considering the increasing coexistence of asthma & sleep apnea, a condition in part characterized by a patient ceasing to breathe for short periods of time while asleep.




Insufficient sleep has been associated with increased risks of heart disease, hypertension (high blood pressure), diabetes, & other cardio-metabolic diseases, with the potential to lead to an early death.



These are just a few examples of the many health conditions that may impact a woman's sleep.

Sleep Tips

Though it may be challenging or impossible to change many intersectional contributors to sleep disparities, educating oneself on the interaction of sleep & health outcomes can be an empowering first step. If you are suffering from considerable sleep complaints, please visit your doctor & discuss the possibility of referral to a sleep physician - quality sleep is essential to leading a healthy & happy life, & is a basic right, not a luxury.

Below we suggest a few behavioral & interpersonal tips you can try to help maximize your sleep quality.


TIP 1: Evaluate your nighttime electronic usage.



Electronic devices emit blue light, which studies have shown can have an alerting effect on the brain. Limiting artificial light exposure at night by putting away your electronic devices as you prepare for bed can help your body to naturally feel more sleepy, rather than awake, when you're ready to go to sleep.

TIP 2: Consider your relationship.



Women who reported being very satisfied with their relationship with their partner also reported having good sleep every night or almost every night. If you aren’t having very good sleep right now, consider how your interpersonal relationships may be impacting your sleep, especially if you share a bed with your partner. Unresolved issues with a partner may result in worrying or anxiety at bedtime, or may manifest in less-than-ideal sleeping situations (e.g., if partners are fighting & one is asked to sleep on the couch instead of in the bed).

TIP 3: Aim for consistent sleep & eating schedules.




Women who maintain consistent timing for meals & sleeping report having better sleep quality. Eating at regular intervals is key to quality sleep, because as Professor Sophie Wehrens & her colleagues have found, meal times play an important role in guiding the human circadian rhythm. Establishing routines around sleeping & waking up similarly entrain the circadian rhythm, which can easily be disrupted by inconsistencies. Drs. Jiunn-Horng Kang and Shih-Ching Chen have suggested that people with erratic sleep patterns may be more prone to poor quality sleep, because they are more likely to have their sleep disturbed by light & noise (e.g., during daytime hours).

About this Website

This website was created as the final project for a Data Visualization course at U.C. Berkeley School of Information (Spring 2019) by Ashten Bartz, Jihea Moon, & Melrose Huang. Our intention is to raise awareness for existing inequalities in sleep quality between different genders, races, & various socioeconomic backgrounds, & their potential negative impacts on health.
