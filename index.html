Women & Sleep



According to researchers at the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute, quality sleep is pivotal to several aspects of physical and mental well-being, such as immune system functioning and memory formation. However, the Center for Disease Control and Prevention has found that only one-third of adults get the sleep they need to maintain healthy bodies and minds. While it is common knowledge that sleep is important, there remains a lack of public awareness around how sleep affects women in particular. Strikingly, for example, the Office on Women’s Health reports that women are more likely than men to experience sleep issues such as insomnia and restless leg syndrome. We hope to shed a light on how women experience sleep and sleep disorders.

Gender impacts Sleep

According to a survey on sleep habits , women reported more issues with their sleep than did men.




Women report higher usage of prescription sleep aids, perhaps in an attempt to mitigate some of these sleep complaints.



These sleep issues may become worse as a woman ages, and over the course of her lifetime, these challenges contribute to significant sleep loss.




Who Searches "insomnia" on Google?



People living in the South Eastern area in the US search “insomnia” more than people in other areas. Various demographic factors in the area (ex. lower income, higher African American population, more female population, etc.) might be correlated.

Sleep & Obesity

Women who are overweight or obese are more likely to have a sleep disorder than women who are normal weight.



There is a bidirectional relationship between sleep and obesity - those who are already obese are more likely to have poorer sleep, and those with poorer sleep are more likely to become obese.

[image of food and exercise?]

Diet and exercise habits directly contribute to body weight, but women who get less than the recommended amount of sleep report being too sleepy to eat healthy foods or too sleepy to exercise.


Sleep & Finances

Women living in low-income households (less than $25k) are especially likely to report disturbed sleep as a result of worrying about costs related to rent, gas, or food.





Of these women who worry about financial matters at bedtime, the low income women take longer to fall asleep.
